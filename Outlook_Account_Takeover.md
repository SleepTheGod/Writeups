Executive Summary
This report details a long-standing vulnerability in the Microsoft Account password recovery workflow that enables unauthorized actors to bypass standard verification and gain control of target accounts. The exploit relies on a combination of social engineering (gathering personal details and contacts) and a potential race condition triggered by simultaneous form submissions across browser tabs. Despite Microsoft's classification of this as social engineering, the core issue appears to involve inconsistent handling of reset code delivery, resulting in unhashed or unintended disclosure of recovery information to an attacker-controlled email address.
The method has reportedly been effective since 2011 and remains functional as of recent testing. Successful exploitation allows full account takeover, including access to associated services like Skype, Teams, Xbox, and Outlook. Immediate mitigation is recommended to prevent widespread abuse.
Detailed Description
The vulnerability occurs during the account recovery process when a user (or attacker) navigates through verification options and reaches the "Recover your account" form. By providing accurate target information (e.g., name, location, and contacts) and submitting the recovery request in rapid succession across multiple tabs, the system allegedly experiences a glitch. This results in the delivery of a full, unstarted password reset code directly to the attacker's specified contact email, bypassing standard security checks.
Microsoft's response to prior reports has framed this as social engineering due to the prerequisite information gathering. However, the inconsistent reset code handling (e.g., unhashing or misdelivery) suggests an underlying technical flaw in session management or email queuing.
Prerequisites for Exploitation
To execute this attack, the adversary must first perform reconnaissance:

Obtain the target's Microsoft account email address.
Collect accurate personal details: first name, last name, city, state, and ZIP code.
Identify at least three contacts from the target's Skype or Microsoft Teams friends list.

These steps enable the attacker to convincingly populate recovery forms, increasing the likelihood of system acceptance.
Steps to Reproduce
The following steps outline a successful exploitation attempt. Reproduction requires a test Microsoft account with known details and contacts. Warning: Testing on production accounts may trigger security alerts or account locks.

Initiate Recovery

Navigate to https://account.live.com/resetpassword.aspx.
Enter the target's email address in the provided field and click "Next."


Bypass Initial Verification

The system prompts for authenticator app code (e.g., "Enter the code generated by your authenticator app").

Alternatively, select "I don't have any of these" to proceed.

In vulnerable scenarios, the reset email arrives with the complete code (e.g., not masked or hashed), enabling immediate use.

Impact

Scope: All Microsoft Accounts with linked Skype/Teams contacts and incomplete 2FA enforcement during recovery.
Confidentiality: Full exposure of account credentials and linked data (e.g., emails, files in OneDrive, Xbox profiles).
Integrity/Availability: Attacker can alter account settings, add recovery options, or lock out the legitimate owner.
Real-World Exploitation: Reportedly used to compromise high-profile accounts (e.g., YouTube channels and Xbox profiles). Widespread potential for phishing, identity theft, and lateral movement to enterprise Microsoft 365 environments.
CVSS v3.1 Score (Estimated): 8.1 (High) – AV:N/AC:H/PR:N/UI:N/S:U/C:H/I:H/A:N (Network access, high complexity due to recon, no privileges required, high impact on confidentiality/integrity).

Mitigation Recommendations

Immediate

Enforce stricter rate-limiting on recovery form submissions to prevent race conditions (e.g., lockout after 1-2 attempts per session/IP).
Ensure all reset codes are consistently hashed/masked in transit and only delivered to verified owner contacts.


Short-Term

Mandate 2FA for all recovery paths; block recovery if 2FA is enabled without hardware token verification.
Validate contact details (e.g., Skype friends) against account ownership proofs beyond self-reported data.


Long-Term

Implement session binding to prevent multi-tab exploits.
Enhance reconnaissance resistance by limiting public exposure of Skype/Teams contacts.
Conduct automated fuzzing of recovery workflows to detect similar glitches.
